# Customer Journey Framework

Use this framework to analyze customer journey evidence without over-crediting last-click performance.

## Journey stages

| Stage | Typical behavior | Useful evidence | Common channels |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acquisition / Discovery | First visit, first exposure, broad audience entry | New users, first-user channel, landing page, engaged sessions | Paid Social, Video, Display, Organic Social, Non-brand Search, Referral, PR |
| Consideration | Return visits, product/category/content research, comparison | Multiple sessions, content engagement, add_to_cart, form_start, page depth | Organic Search, Email, Paid Social, Display retargeting, Referral, Direct |
| Demand Capture | User expresses existing intent | Brand Search, high-intent Non-brand Search, Direct, product/category landing pages | Paid Search, Organic Search, Direct, Shopping, Local |
| Conversion Closing | Final touchpoint before purchase/lead/sign-up | Last session, purchase/lead event, checkout or form completion | Direct, Paid Search, Email, Retargeting, Affiliate, SMS |
| Retention / Re-engagement | Repeat purchase, renewal, repeat lead, returning user activation | Returning users, repeat transactions, lifecycle email/SMS, CRM audiences | Email, SMS, Direct, Organic Search, Retargeting |

## Path analysis logic

Look for:

- Top conversion paths by users, conversions, and revenue.
- High-value paths that may have lower volume but higher revenue/order value.
- Short paths: often high-intent, brand, direct, remarketing, or returning-user journeys.
- Long paths: often research-heavy or high-consideration journeys.
- Common entry touchpoints: first path nodes, first-user channel, landing page.
- Common middle touchpoints: repeated consideration channels, product/content pages, return sessions.
- Common closing touchpoints: final path nodes before conversion.
- Path fragmentation: many small path variants can indicate complex consideration, tracking issues, or inconsistent campaign tagging.

## Overlap interpretation

High overlap can mean:

- Healthy reinforcement: upper-funnel channel introduces users and lower-funnel channel captures demand.
- Necessary multi-touch journey: high-consideration products need repeated touches.
- Wasteful duplication: multiple paid channels repeatedly hit the same users without incremental benefit.
- Audience cannibalization: retargeting, coupon, affiliate, or brand search captures users who were already likely to convert.

Judge overlap by pairing it with:

- New-user rate.
- First-user channel vs session channel.
- Conversion and revenue quality.
- Frequency and recency where available.
- Campaign type: prospecting vs retargeting, brand vs non-brand, coupon vs content/partner.
- Landing page intent.

## Contribution interpretation

Classify a channel based on evidence, not platform labels:

- First-user share and new-user rate indicate acquisition contribution.
- Frequent early path appearance indicates demand generation or discovery.
- Frequent middle path appearance indicates consideration support.
- Frequent final path appearance indicates demand capture or conversion closing.
- High last-click revenue with low new-user share can indicate capture of existing demand.
- Low last-click revenue with high upstream appearance, engagement, or downstream Search/Direct conversion can indicate under-credited assistance.

## Business implications to surface

- Budget allocation should not be based only on last-click ROAS.
- Brand Search should be separated from Non-brand Search where possible.
- Retargeting and Affiliate should be checked for incrementality.
- Upper-funnel channels should use KPIs such as new-user rate, engaged sessions, assisted paths, branded search lift, direct return lift, and qualified downstream conversions.
- Direct should be treated as a mixed bucket containing known demand, returning users, dark traffic, cookie loss, untagged campaigns, and true direct navigation.
